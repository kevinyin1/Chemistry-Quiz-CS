Atomic Structure

The Atom

Originally thought of as the smallest indivisible particle, the atom is the basic building block of matter; it is considered the samllest particle of an element that retains all characteristics of the element. The atom, as we know it today, has gone through many models and has become universally accepted by the electron-cloud model. In this model the atom's structure follows:

  1. The atom's atomic mass is stored in the nucleus or center of the atom
  2. The nucleus is comprised of protons and nuetrons
  3. An electron cloud, comprised of mostly empty space and electrons, surrounds the atom

Periodicity

The Periodic table of Elements

At first glance the periodic table of elements is arranged by an elements most basic characteristic: its atomic number also known as the number of protons. However, delving deeper we noticed that the periodic table has trends we refer to in Chemisty as periodicity. To understand periodicity, we need a basic understanding of the periodic table of elements.

  1. Periods
    Periods are the horizontal rows in the periodic table; they run from left to right. There are seven periods in total. Periodicity in periods:
    1. Electronegativity becomes increasingly negative as we go across a period
    2. Atomic radius decrease from left to right
    3. First ionization energy increases from left to right
    4. Electron infinity increases as we go across a period
    5. Non-metallic character increases from left to right of a period
  2. Groups
    Groups are the vertical columns in the periodic table. There are a total of eighteen.
    • Group 1 elements are alkali metals
    • Group 2 elements are alkaline earth metals
    • Groups 3-12 elements are transition metals
    • Group 13 is known are the Boron family
    • Group 14 is known are the Carbon family
    • Group 15 is known are the Nitrogen family
    • Group 16 is known are the Oxygen family
    • Group 17 elements are halogens
    • Group 18 elements are noble gases
    • The Periodicity in a group is as follows:
      1. Electronegativity decreases as we go down a group
      2. Atomic radius increases as we go down a group
      3. First ionization decreases as we go down a group
      4. Electron affinity decreases as we go down a group
      5. Metallic character becomes more apparent as we go down a group

Bonding

Bonding is a phenomenon that occurs between elements that do not have a stable octet- eight
electrons in the outermost shell; in essence, they need to have a noble gas electron configuration. In fact, bonding occurs between almost every element to obtain a stable
eight valence electrons.There are three mains types of bonds: ionic, covalent, and metallic.

  1. Ionic Bonds
    1. Ionic bonds occur between a non-metal element and a metal element. To be even more specific, the electronegativity difference between the two elements must be at least 1.7.
    2. Since the electronegativity difference is so great. The electron is actually not "shared" but is pulled by the more electronegative element(the element which has a greater affinity for electrons), which in most cases is the non-metal. As a result, one element is negatively charge while the other is positively charged.
    3. Example: Sodium(Na) and Florine(F) bonds together so that each element now has 8 valence electrons. The electron is pulled by florine becasue it is the more elctronegative.
  2. Covalent bonds - There are two types of covalent bonds: Non-polar covalent and polar covalent
    1. Covalent bonds occur between metals and metals, non-metals and non-metals, or metalloids and metalloids. Essentially, the electronegativity difference between the two elements must be less than 1.7.
      1. Non-polar covalent bonds usually only occur between the same element. However, in rare cases the also occur between elements whose electronegativities are less than 0.4.
        1. The electron is shared loosely in non-polar covalent bonds. As a result, neither element is more charged than the other
        2. Example: F-F
      2. Polar Covalent bonds occur between elements taht have a electronegativity differencebetween 0.4 to 1.7.
        1. The electron here is pulled more closely to the more electronegative element. However, it is only slightly charged.
        2. In a polar covalent bond, the more electronegative element is slightly negative while the other isslightly positive.
        3. Example: C-Cl4
    2. Metallic Bonds - usually occur between the transition metal elements
      1. A metallic bond occurs between two metals. This bond is unique because electrons are not shared but almost lose completely. In fact, the electron is able to freely 'swim' around the compound formed by the two elements
      2. In a metallic bond the both elements are positively charged.
    3. Note about all bonds: Sometimes the bonded elements may need to share more than one electron as a result it forms a double bond. The second bond follows the exact same properties as those listed above. However, when a double bond is formed, the distance between the two atoms drastically descreases.

Atomic Numbers


In addition to the perioodicity of a periodic table, the elements are formatted in elemental squares. These squares usually have:
  • The element's symbol
  • The atomic number or the number of protons
  • The atomic mass - the sum of the number or nuetrons and protons
  • Electron configuration
  1. The atomic number of a chemical element is the number of protons in its nucleus
    • Notice that the atomic number is identical to the charge of the nucleus.
  2. The atomic mass is is the same of the number of nuetrons and protons
    • The atomic mass should not be confused with the atomic number.
    • Elements have different isotopes(atoms with differnet number of nuetrons and therefore have a different atomic mass)
  3. The atomic number is the most important aspect of the element.
    • It relates the number of protons to the number of electrons
    • It dictates atommic charge
    • It differentiates differents elements
    • Atoms in the periodic table are arranged by its atomic number
    • The atomic number controls its metallic/non-metallic character

Groups

After the periodic table was arranged by atomic number. Each column was given a name. More importantly, each group or column's elements share similiar chemical properties.

Each group has its own unique name and characteristics:

  1. Group 1 elements are alkali metals
    1. They are the first group in the periodic table
    2. Alkali metals bond with a +1 oxidation state - charge
    3. Have the lowest ionization energy
    4. They are violently reactive with other elements
    5. Are never found naturally alone in nature
  2. Group 2 elements are alkaline earth metals
    1. They are the second group in the periodic table
    2. Alkaline earth metals metals bond with a +2 oxidation state - charge
    3. Have the second lowest ionization energy
    4. High boiling points and melting points
    5. HAve low densities
  3. Groups 3-12 elements are transition metals
    1. These elements have multiple oxidation states; they're oxidatiion state is determined by what they bond with and the octet rule
    2. THye form coloured compounds
    3. They are the first group in the periodic table
    4. Have high melting points
    5. Are good conductors of heat and electricity
  4. Group 13 is known are the Boron family
    1. These elements have an oxidation state of +3
    2. This group starts off the metlloid trend>
    3. All elements within this group are lewis elements
  5. Group 14 is known are the Carbon family
    1. Have oxidations states of either +4 or -4
    2. Have unique lewis dot structures - one electron per s and p orbital
    3. These elements, along with the noble gases, are the only elements that can be found pure in nature
  6. Group 15 is known are the Nitrogen family
    1. Have oxidation states of -3
    2. These elements are most commonly used for fertilizers and explosives
  7. Group 16 is known are the Oxygen family
    1. Have oxidation states of -2
    2. Metallic character increases as you travel down the family
  8. Group 17 elements are halogens
    1. These elements form a -1 bond
    2. They are violently reactive especially with alkali and alkaline earth metals
    3. Are generally poisonous
  9. Group 18 elements are noble gases
    1. These elements rarely, if ever, form bonds
    2. Have a full octet
    4. Exist in the gaseous form

Metals, Metalloids and Non-metals

Elements of the periodic table are classified as metals, metalloids, and non-metals. Metals comprise the majority of elements and are found towards the left of the periodic table. Metalloids are found in groups thirteen to seventeen. Non-metals are located towards the right of the peirodic table.

  1. Characteristics of Metals
    1. Are are good conductors of heat and electricity
    2. Are Malleable - can be beaten into sheets
    3. Are ductile - stretched into wires
    4. Exhibits a metallic luster
  2. Characterisitcs of Metalloids
    1. Are semi-conductors
    2. Are solids at rooom temperature
    3. Their oxidation states can range from +3 to -2
    4. Tend to share electrons when they react with other substances
    5. Their physical Characterisitics resemble metals, while their chemical characteristics resemble those of non-metallic elements
  3. Characteristics of Non-Metals
    1. Are poor conductors of heat and electricity
    2. Nonductile - cannot be stretched into wires
    3. Are generally brittle especially if at room temperature
    4. Do not possess metallic lusters
